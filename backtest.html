Sober forward expectations

Both backtests are bull-tilted — most of each sample fell in rising markets, so forward expectations are deliberately haircut for selection bias:
  • Returns: expect roughly half the backtested annual figures live.
  • Sharpe: backtested ~1.2–1.9 → sober forward ~0.8–1.3.
  • Drawdown: plan for ~2× the backtested max drawdown.
Pre-registered kill criteria: retire a deployment if any of — 6-month forward Sharpe < 0, OR profit factor < 1.05, OR realized max drawdown > 2× backtest.

Validation tests passed

  1. Walk-forward (train early years, test later years): out-of-sample profit factor stronger than in-sample.
  2. Sensitivity: deliberately scrambling the logic collapses returns to ~$0 — confirms the edge is real, not curve-fit.
  3. Bootstrap 95% CIs exclude break-even on profit factor, Sharpe and net P&L.
  4. Beats simpler baselines on Sharpe, net P&L and drawdown simultaneously.
  5. The two strategies are nearly uncorrelated, so combining them raises the portfolio Sharpe above either alone.

Honest caveats

  • The two strategies are shown on their own live sizings — Strategy A at a fixed 1 NQ (= 10 MNQ), Strategy B at its live MNQ sizing (0.5% risk ≈ $830/trade), both expressed on a $100k base — so their dollar P&L figures are not directly comparable; compare them on profit factor, Sharpe and return %.
  • ~5 years of data, most of it in rising markets; only one true bear stretch in each sample.
  • Many parameters were explored during research, so some selection bias is inevitable.
  • The forward paper-trading demo is the only remaining honest, fully out-of-sample validator.
  • Strategy A1 is the long-option (defined-risk) expression of a directional component of Strategy A over a ~2-year history and is a backtest only (not yet live) — its edge is mostly capped downside, so compare on annualized return and drawdown, not raw dollars.
  • Strategy B1 is the option expression of Strategy B over a shorter (~2-year) history and is a backtest only (not yet live) — its higher return reflects defined-risk option convexity plus the recent window; compare on annualized return, not raw dollars.
